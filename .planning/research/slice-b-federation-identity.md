# Slice B — Federated Messaging + DID / SSI Landscape vs. wire

Pre-investment due diligence on wire's neighbors. Two categories: (3) federated messaging protocols that ship signed-event infrastructure for humans, (5) DID / self-sovereign-identity stacks that ship portable cryptographic identity primitives. Wire is **not** a competitor to most of these — it's an agent-to-agent message bus that happens to reuse a small subset of their primitives (Ed25519 keypairs, signed events, federated handle resolution). The interesting question is what each project's adoption arc teaches us about wire's own.

Sources are cited inline as `[tier, source, age-band]` where tier = P/S/T (primary / secondary / tertiary), age-band = `fresh` (<3mo), `recent` (3-12mo), `aging` (1-3yr), `stale` (>3yr).

**Per-category notes**

- **Matrix's federation reader regularly pegs CPU at 100% for "no explicable reason"** [P, github.com/matrix-org/synapse#10698, aging] and Element introduced *Synapse Pro* in Jan 2025 explicitly because OSS Synapse couldn't scale to "massive deployments" [S, element.io/blog, recent]. That's the cost of room-state-resolution federation at scale, and it's directly why wire chose mailbox-per-pair (no shared state, no resolution problem) instead of room-state.
- **Mastodon's MAU is *down* from 2.6M (Nov 2022) to ~750k–1M (Apr 2026)** despite registered growth to 10.5M [S, marketful.com, recent] — registration ≠ retention. Bluesky's ~5.3M MAU on a single mostly-centralized AppView dwarfs the entire Fediverse's ~2M MAU.
- **Nostr** has working spam mitigation via NIP-13 PoW + NIP-57 zaps [P, github.com/nostr-protocol/nips, recent], but the cost is "relay operators are forced to implement anti-spam measures … which further complicates the user experience." Wire avoids this entirely because the bilateral-consent gate means there is no "stranger can write to your inbox" attack surface to defend.
- **Bluesky / ATproto** is the only project in this category currently operating a working DID-based identity system at scale (~40M registered, ~5.3M MAU) — and it had to invent did:plc to do it because no other DID method was deployable [P, atproto.com, recent]. The independent did:plc operator org being spun up in early 2026 [S, atprotocol.dev, recent] is the same kind of governance hand-off Inrupt did with Solid → ODI. Both teams concluded that protocol-as-foundation is the only way to escape "this is a vendor product" stigma.
- **XMPP** is the textbook case of how federation dies: Google embraced it in 2006, weaponized TLS-required S2S to break interop, then killed federation entirely in 2013-15 [S, kirsle.net, aging]. Facebook never federated. End result — XMPP "exists" (still active XSF, FOSDEM 2026 talks) but consumer mindshare collapsed. The killer wasn't a technical flaw; it was *no end user knew the network existed* [S, kirsle.net, aging] — the brand belonged to Google Talk, not Jabber.
- **IRCv3** is the survival-mode arc: rather than try to displace IRC, it incrementally added SASL, capability negotiation, message-tags, reactions, replies [P, ircv3.net, fresh]. Real adoption is uneven but the spec is shipped and respected. Roughly the path "smaller, additive, low-leverage" that wire is on.
- **Solid** is the cautionary "PhD-supervisor protocol" case: technically sound (RDF graphs + WebID), backed by Berners-Lee, real pilots (BBC/NHS/NatWest/Flanders), but no consumer base 8 years in, and Inrupt itself classifies hosted PodSpaces as "developer preview, not for production" [P, inrupt.com/blog/pod-spaces-upgrade, recent]. Inrupt handed governance back to the Open Data Institute in Oct 2024 — a sign the commercial pull was insufficient.

**Per-category notes**

- **Microsoft Entra removed did:ion as a trust system option in Dec 2023, leaving did:web as the only choice** [P, learn.microsoft.com/en-us/entra/verified-id/whats-new, recent]. Microsoft's *own* DID network — the marquee SSI deployment of 2021–2023, marketed as the public-permissionless future — got walked back to "anchor your key in a domain's `.well-known/did.json`." That is a near-perfect microcosm of where the broader SSI movement landed: the heavyweight, blockchain-anchored, fully-decentralized methods lost to the lightweight, domain-anchored ones because operating cost collapsed and the marginal trust gain was invisible to end users.
- **W3C VC 2.0 + OID4VC + mDL (ISO 18013-5) is where SSI is *actually* shipping at tens-of-millions scale** [P, w3.org press release 2025, recent] — and the user *doesn't know it exists*. EU EUDI Wallet, US state mDLs, Europass credentials. The wins are happening, just packaged as "digital driver's license" not as "SSI." Wire's lesson here: brand the user-visible thing (the message bus, the handle), not the cryptographic substrate.
- **did:plc ≈ "we needed it for production so we built it"** — Bluesky tried did:ion / did:peer / did:web and none of them survived contact with "8M users migrating handles between PDSes." The team published the method, is now spinning up an independent org to operate the directory [S, atprotocol.dev/bluesky-and-did-plc, recent]. The shape — small registry-of-handles, per-DID rotation log, key controls the DID — is exactly the shape wire converged on for `wireup.net/handles/<nick>`.
- **Spruce / KILT / Veramo** are the "we built SSI plumbing, who will deploy it?" tier. Spruce has the most traction (state-gov mDL contracts in CA/UT) precisely because they stopped selling "self-sovereign identity" and started selling "the boring digital ID glue your DMV needs."
- **Adoption barriers explicitly identified in the SSI literature** [S, helpnetsecurity 2025, recent; S, dock.io/post/self-sovereign-identity, recent]: (a) key-loss = identity-loss with no "forgot password," (b) every verifier has a different API so integration cost scales with N², (c) incumbents profit from owning identity and won't disrupt themselves, (d) without consumer demand, businesses don't push for it, (e) "credential recovery remains unsolved at scale." Four out of five of these are addressable for *agent* identity in a way they aren't for *human* identity (agents tolerate redeploy + re-pair, operators back up keypairs as part of normal ops, there's no consumer market to convince), which is the deeper reason wire's tighter scope is defensible.

## Cross-category patterns and adoption-inertia observations

Six adoption-inertia patterns recur across both categories and are directly load-bearing for wire's roadmap:

1. **The user can't see the federation, so they can't defend it.** XMPP's death wasn't TLS politics; it was that "Google Talk users" never knew they were Jabber users [S, kirsle.net, aging]. When Google withdrew, there was no constituency to mourn or migrate. Wire's analog: if `wire send` is what agents call but operators never see the `nick@wireup.net` handle, there's no constituency for the protocol when wireup.net inevitably faces a sustainability question. Mitigation: keep the handle visible in agent-output (it currently is), keep the relay URL pasteable, never let "wire" become "Slancha's agent comms feature."

2. **Operational cost decides the method, not the spec quality.** did:ion was a more "decentralized" method than did:web in every academic sense — and Microsoft turned it off [P, learn.microsoft.com, recent]. Solid is technically purer than Mastodon and has nothing close to Mastodon's user base. Matrix's purer federation design imposes 100%-CPU-federation-reader pain [P, github.com/matrix-org/synapse#10698, aging] that vendors are now monetizing via Synapse Pro [S, element.io, recent]. **Operational simplicity wins.** Wire's single-Rust-binary + 256-KiB-cap + mailbox-not-state-machine choice is on the right side of this lesson.

3. **Centralized registries inside decentralized protocols are not a sin — they are how this stuff ships.** did:plc admits "we have a central directory" and operates Bluesky [P, atproto.com, recent]. Inrupt was Solid's central commercial entity for 6 years. wireup.net is wire's central handle directory. The play is *operator-portable* centralization (you can switch relays, you can keep your key, the directory is replaceable), not "no central anything." Wire already does this — the `--relay` flag is the escape valve. Document it louder.

4. **Bilateral consent + mailbox model dodges entire spam/abuse problem classes.** Mastodon needs blocklist subscriptions, scheduled syncs, bulk domain imports [S, dasroot.net, recent]. Nostr needs PoW + Lightning zaps + per-relay-policy [P, github.com/nostr-protocol/nips, recent]. ActivityPub spends large slices of every release on moderation tooling. Wire never needs to because the bilateral pair gate means "stranger can write to your inbox" simply isn't an attack surface. This is wire's biggest architectural moat against scope creep into anti-abuse tooling — keep it.

5. **The pivot from "SSI" to "agent identity" is the same shape as the pivot from "decentralized web" to "boring digital wallet plumbing."** SSI's actual production wins are EU EUDI Wallet + state mDLs + Europass — none of which use the language of "self-sovereign" [P, w3.org/press-releases/2025, recent]. They use the language of "your government ID, on your phone, that you don't have to hand over to verify your age." Wire's analog: don't lead with "federated agent protocol"; lead with "your agents can talk to each other and you keep the keys." The first is a sales pitch, the second is a problem statement.

6. **DID-method proliferation is a warning, not a goal.** There are at least 100+ registered DID methods; the ones that are deployed at scale (did:web, did:plc, did:key) are all the conceptually-cheap ones. The blockchain-anchored ones (did:ion, did:btcr, did:ethr-as-primary-identity) consistently lose. Wire should NOT rush to register `did:wire` as its own method — if anything, presenting wire's identifiers as did:web with a wire-specific service endpoint (`#wire-mailbox`) would inherit far more tooling than minting a new method that nobody indexes. Worth a roadmap line.

The Bluesky / ATproto comparison is the most useful single data point: ~40M registered accounts, ~5.3M MAU, did:plc invented from scratch because every existing method failed in production, governance being handed to an independent operator org in early 2026, IETF working-group charter Jan 2026 [P, atproto.com, recent]. That arc (build the thing → reach scale → realize you invented a new DID method → spin governance into an external org → submit to IETF) is the realistic best-case trajectory for what wire is doing, just at agent-coordination scale instead of social-graph scale.

The lesson is not "be Bluesky." The lesson is: when wire reaches the point where wireup.net hosts handles for thousands of distinct operators, the choice between (a) "Slancha runs it forever" and (b) "spin an independent org" is going to be the same choice Bluesky made, and the protocol design should make (b) survivable. It currently does — handles are per-relay, keys live with the operator, the relay is AGPL Rust. Worth not regressing on.
