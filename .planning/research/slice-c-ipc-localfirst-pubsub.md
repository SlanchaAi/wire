# Slice C — Local IPC, Local-First/CRDT, and Pub/Sub Landscape vs. wire

Pre-investment due diligence on the OSS + commercial neighbors of wire's *transport* layer (as opposed to slice B's *identity* layer). Three categories: (4) local IPC + service discovery — the incumbents wire's UDS transport will quietly compete with on the same machine; (6) local-first / CRDT systems with optional federation — architecturally the closest cousins (device-local + cross-device sync); (8) commercial pub/sub with multi-tenant identity — wire's federated mailbox shape in its industrialized form. wire is *not* trying to compete head-on with NATS or Kafka, but it inherits or rejects design choices from all three categories, and the closing paragraph asks the honest question: are we reinventing solved problems?

Sources are cited inline as `[tier, source, age-band]` where tier = P/S/T (primary / secondary / tertiary), age-band = `fresh` (<3mo), `recent` (3-12mo), `aging` (1-3yr), `stale` (>3yr).

**Per-category notes**

- **Linux IPC is in active migration.** Lennart Poettering pushed systemd toward Varlink because "D-Bus didn't make it into the kernel and Varlink is simpler" [P, phoronix.com/news/Systemd-Varlink-D-Bus-Future, fresh]. Systemd v260 ships a Varlink service registry [P, linuxiac.com, fresh]. wire's UDS transport landing in v0.7 should expose a Varlink interface as a tier-1 deliverable; on Linux that gets agents service-discovery for free without wire writing a daemon.
- **NATS JetStream is the project wire most resembles industrially.** Subjects = mailboxes, accounts = bilateral-pair-equivalent tenancy, JetStream KV/Object = the durability layer wire deliberately doesn't ship. The case for wire-over-NATS exists — Synadia even pitches "heterogeneous agents, one fabric" [P, synadia.com/blog/heterogeneous-agents-one-fabric, recent]. The case *against* is operational: a `cargo install` single binary vs a cluster, AGPL vs Apache (we own the upstream), bilateral-pair-pin as the trust gate vs JWT-issuance-to-accounts. Synadia's NATS-graduation stall + maintainer "100% yea to leave CNCF" vote [S, luxantsolutions.com, fresh] is the political weather around the project — adoption is fine but governance is fragile.
- **gRPC vs Cap'n Proto vs ZeroMQ is the wrong axis for wire.** gRPC adds ~100µs of HTTP/2+protobuf overhead per unary call locally [S, www.mpi-hd.mpg.de/personalhomes/fwerner, aging], ZeroMQ outperforms it but forces the user to invent framing/identity [S, bbengfort.github.io, stale], Cap'n Proto wins on schema + capability story but needs a code generation step. wire is async messaging not RPC; the right axis is "subject bus + signed envelope," which puts NATS + MQTT in the comparison set, not gRPC.
- **Bonjour/mDNS as an opt-in discovery layer is cheap.** A 50-line addition to `wire daemon` publishes `_wire._tcp.local` with TXT pointing at the local-relay slot. Operators on the same LAN (not just same uid) discover each other zero-config. Risk: LAN-side spoofing of TXT records. Mitigation: the published record only carries a pubkey hint, not a capability — the bilateral pair gate still runs.
- **Apple XPC is the platform-native wire-shaped thing on macOS.** It's also the case study for why wire shouldn't try to *be* XPC: XPC connections are Mach-port-backed and tied to launchd-spawned helpers; the API is the OS. Wire's UDS transport mimics the *shape* (sibling-process trusted IPC, capability-style) without inheriting the platform lock-in.

**Per-category notes**

- **Yjs has won the CRDT mindshare war.** ~21.8k★ vs Automerge's ~6.3k, ~4.4M weekly npm downloads vs Automerge's ~7k [S, npmtrends.com, fresh]. The technical case for Automerge (clean op-log, JSON-shaped) keeps losing to Yjs's ecosystem (Tiptap, Quill, ProseMirror, Monaco). The lesson for wire: **the framework that owns the editor integration wins, not the one with the cleaner spec.** Wire's analog: own the MCP-tool integration story end-to-end (Claude Code, Cursor, Continue, Aider), not the protocol-spec PDF.
- **Iroh's 1.0-rc landing the same week as Tether's Holepunch grants program** [P, iroh.computer/blog, fresh; P, tether.io/news, fresh] is the same kind of "the P2P infrastructure layer is finally ready" inflection point we've been hearing about for a decade. Both projects ship a single-binary runtime, both use pubkey-as-identity, both run a fallback relay for NAT-traversal failures. **Iroh's `iroh-relay` is structurally what wire-relay is**, modulo the message shape (QUIC bytes vs. signed JSONL envelopes). If wire ever feels HTTP+relay friction at scale, iroh is the off-the-shelf transport to consider.
- **Tonk is the AI-agent local-first project worth tracking.** "Portable data substrate for humans and their tools to think together" [P, github.com/tonk-labs/tonk, fresh] is the same problem statement wire has, just from the state-store angle instead of the message-bus angle. The two are composable, not competitive — wire ships diffs between Tonk databases.
- **Hypercore/Holepunch + Tether grants** is interesting funding optics. Tether is now actively paying developers to build on Pear/Hypercore [P, tether.io/news, fresh]. The "centralized crypto exchange funds decentralized infra" pattern is the same shape as Bluesky-funded did:plc; both are operator-portable centralization. Wire's "wireup.net + AGPL relay" structure is conceptually identical.
- **IPFS + libp2p's 2026 governance split into independent foundations** [P, protocol.ai/blog, recent] is the maturity arc wire is too early for but should be designed to enable. Single-org governance is fine at v0.x; the question is whether the protocol survives the inevitable handoff. Wire's three-license trio (AGPL relay + Apache spec + MIT CLI) is structurally on the right side.
- **Pijul, Earthstar, Tonk are the "technically-pure, market-anemic" tier.** Beautiful protocols, no scaled deployment. Adopt their ideas, don't adopt their adoption strategy.

**Per-category notes**

- **Pusher → Ably → Centrifugo → Self-Host is the arc.** Ably runs an entire site comparing itself to Pusher and aggressively converting customers [P, ably.com/compare/ably-vs-pusher, fresh]. Centrifugo lists Pusher + Ably + PubNub + Phoenix.PubSub + SignalR all as "things we replace" [P, github.com/centrifugal/centrifugo, fresh]. Operators have been migrating off hosted realtime for cost + control for a decade, and the OSS server is now production-credible. Wire is one tier further down the trend (no broker at all from the operator's perspective, just `wire send`), and the same operator demand is what makes it viable.
- **MQTT signed payloads + bilateral pair is the right reference frame for wire's threat model.** MQTT's docs explicitly cover "the sender signs the message with its private key and the receiver validates the stamp with the public key of the sending client" [P, hivemq.com/blog/mqtt-security-fundamentals-mqtt-message-data-integrity, recent] — but MQTT does this *on top of* topic-based ACLs because anyone can publish to any topic they're authorized for. Wire collapses both layers: bilateral pair is the *only* write-grant, and the per-event sig validates the asserted sender. Less stuff, fewer attack surfaces.
- **Kafka and Pulsar are not in wire's weight class.** They're petabyte-scale streaming with multi-DC replication; wire is single-machine to small-mesh coordination. The relevant lesson is multi-tenancy *shape*: Pulsar's `tenant > namespace > topic` tree is the clean version of what wire would need if `wireup.net` ever hosts handles for multiple orgs with audit-isolation requirements. Today wire is "one relay, FCFS nicks, per-handle directory entry" — the simplest workable version. Pulsar's tree is the picture of what the relay surface looks like at scale.
- **Centrifugo's recent CVE-2026-32301 SSRF in the dynamic JWKS endpoint** [P, github.com/centrifugal/centrifugo CHANGELOG, fresh] is the kind of bug wire's smaller surface area avoids — wire doesn't fetch JWKS, doesn't have a JWT verifier, doesn't have a callback URL configuration. Less code = fewer vulnerabilities; document this in security posture.
- **RabbitMQ's Khepri migration in v4.3** [P, rabbitmq.com/blog/2026/04/23, fresh] is the case study for "we deprecated the old metadata store, period." Mature OSS can make breaking infra changes at major version bumps and survive. Wire's v0.x → v1.0 will need the same discipline.

## Closing — Are we reinventing solved problems?

Honest answer, in three layers.

**Local IPC: yes, partially — and that's fine.** D-Bus, Varlink, XPC, and NATS *do* solve "processes on one box talking to each other." NATS solves it at GPU-fleet scale [P, synadia.com/blog/scaling-global-ai-inference-with-nats-jetstream, recent]. Varlink is on track to be the systemd-native default on Linux [P, phoronix.com, fresh]. XPC is the only sanctioned option on Apple. Wire is **not** going to out-compete any of these on raw same-box throughput, nor should it try. What wire offers that those don't is **identity continuity**: the same Ed25519 keypair and the same `nick@relay` handle work for the agent on this box, the sister agent next door, and the agent on someone else's laptop across the internet. NATS gets you there too if you stand up a NATS supercluster with decentralized JWT accounts — but that's a cluster + an auth tree + an ops team. Wire's pitch is **the smallest credible identity-plus-transport pair for an individual operator running 3 agents**. NATS is the right answer for an org with a platform team; wire is the right answer for a developer with two Claudes open. Different scale, different operational budget, the same underlying problem shape. The crypto overhead is a real cost (sig-verify per event, key management per session) and we should keep flagging it — but pair-and-sign is the *minimum* cryptographic floor for anything that wants to call itself "transport with identity baked in," and the floor is structurally cheap (Ed25519 verify is sub-microsecond [P, ed25519.cr.yp.to, stale]).

**Local-first + CRDT: no, we're solving an adjacent problem.** Yjs, Automerge, Iroh, Hypercore, Tonk, Earthstar are all solving "device-local state that syncs across devices." Wire is solving "device-local *messages* that move between devices." A wire event can *carry* an Automerge change-set, a Y.js update, an Iroh blob CID, a Tonk triple — wire is the **transport layer underneath**, not a competitor to the state layer. The composition is interesting: wire-event-of-yjs-update gives you (signed-by-sender + bilateral-pair-pinned + multi-machine + offline-capable) shared editing without inventing a new CRDT. The biggest risk in this category isn't that wire competes with Yjs; it's that we **accidentally invent a worse CRDT** by trying to merge agent messages instead of staying message-passing. Don't.

**Pub/sub: no, wire is too small for that lane and intentionally so.** Kafka, Pulsar, RabbitMQ, MQTT solve fan-out broadcast and durable streams at petabyte scale. Pusher and Ably solve hosted realtime for browser clients. Centrifugo is the OSS self-host version of that latter use case. None of them are competitors to wire because wire is **bilateral, not fan-out**; the entire point is "I send to a peer I've explicitly paired with," not "I publish to a topic any subscriber can read." That's a feature, not a missing feature. The closest production analog in this category is *NATS subjects + accounts*, and the relevant industry context is: NATS-graduation from CNCF has stalled because the project is "moving too fast" for foundation governance [S, luxantsolutions.com, fresh]; the maintainers voted 100% to leave. That's an honest weather report on the state of the messaging-bus market — even the most successful incumbent is fighting its own governance. The market is not "well-served and saturated"; it's "well-served at hyperscale and largely unserved for the small-operator agent use case." Wire's slice is the second one.

**Net.** The slice C survey reinforces slice B's punchline: wire's defensibility is *not* "best protocol design," it's **identity-first agent-mesh in the smallest credible operational shape**. The genuine differentiators are (a) bilateral pair as the *only* write-grant (collapses the ACL surface), (b) single binary including the relay (collapses the ops surface), (c) the same identity from anon → local → federation (collapses the rebind surface). The genuine *non*-differentiators are (a) any claim of "best raw throughput" (NATS wins, do not contest), (b) any claim of "best state-sync semantics" (Yjs wins, do not contest), (c) any claim of "production-scale multi-tenant pub-sub" (Pulsar/Kafka own the lane). Stay in our lane, ship the boring identity-first transport, and the slice we *are* solving — operator-grade agent coordination without a vendor — does have real demand and no incumbent that's the obvious winner. The two-week tactical move is to ship a Varlink interface for the UDS transport in v0.7 (free Linux service discovery), an mDNS-publish opt-in for LAN sister discovery in v0.7.x (free LAN discovery without LAN-spoof attack surface), and a NATS-bridge case study showing wire-event-carries-NATS-message for orgs that already run NATS but want operator-pinned identity on top. All three are additive, all three keep the wire surface small, and all three borrow the right ideas from the incumbents in this slice without trying to be them.
